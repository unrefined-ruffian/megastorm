# Identity Persuasion

**What this lens does:** Analyzes a product, feature, or experience as an identity trade — not what it does for the user, but who it lets them become. Stories don't transmit information. They offer identity deals. Products don't solve problems. They grant permission to be a version of yourself.

**The key insight:** "A story is a deal with identity, not with attention." Identification literally changes personality, not just preference. Sports fans, architecture students — identity adoption rewires measurable traits. If your product or experience doesn't offer an identity trade, it's competing on function alone, which means it's competing on price.

## When NOT to Use

- The problem is purely technical or operational — identity mechanics need a human choosing to engage
- The product is internal tooling with captive users who have no identity stake in using it
- JTBD already covered the emotional/social dimensions thoroughly — check whether you're asking "what job is hired?" (JTBD) or "who does the user become?" (identity). Similar but the intervention point is different.

## The Framework

| Dimension | Question |
|---|---|
| **The identity trade** | What version of themselves does the user get to be? What identity are they trading INTO by engaging with this? |
| **The "I am" map** | What does the target audience say after "I am..."? What are their heroes, villains, obstacles? What insider language do they use? |
| **Permission structure** | What identity tension exists? What version of themselves do they need PERMISSION to be? (Premium pricing, exclusive access, and expert endorsement are all permission structures.) |
| **The mentor frame** | Is the product/brand positioned as hero or mentor? Gandalf, not Frodo. "We help people like you win" beats "we are impressive." |
| **Identity backfire risk** | Could this signal an anti-value to part of the audience? Energy bulbs labeled "protect the environment" repelled conservatives. Anti-signal is worse than no signal. |
| **Social proof match** | Is proof coming from people-like-them? Social proof from in-group is 4x more persuasive than generic appeals. The messenger's identity match matters more than the message. |

## How to Run It

1. Map the audience's identity landscape — what do they say after "I am..."? Heroes, villains, insider language.
2. Identify the identity trade the product/experience offers — who do they get to become?
3. Check for permission gaps — is there identity tension the product could resolve?
4. Audit the brand's position — hero or mentor?
5. Scan for backfire risk — could any element signal an anti-value?
6. Check social proof alignment — are testimonials/signals from in-group?

## Agent Prompt

> "Apply the Identity Persuasion framework to this problem brief. Map the audience's identity landscape first — who are they, what do they say after 'I am...', who are their heroes and villains? Then analyze: what identity trade does this product/feature/experience offer? What version of themselves does the user become by engaging?
>
> Check for permission gaps (identity tension the product could resolve), the mentor-vs-hero frame (is the brand positioned correctly?), backfire risk (could any element signal an anti-value?), and social proof alignment (are signals coming from in-group?).
>
> Write your Reframe (what identity trade is actually being offered vs. what the product thinks it's offering), Ideas (interventions that strengthen the identity trade or fix misalignment), and Wild Card (the identity dimension nobody is designing for — the one that would make the product feel like it was made for this specific person)."

## Output Format

```markdown
## Identity Persuasion Output

**The audience's identity map:**
- "I am..." [3-5 identity statements the target audience holds]
- Heroes: [who they admire]
- Villains: [who/what they define themselves against]
- Insider language: [words that signal belonging]

**The identity trade:**
- Current: [What identity does the product currently offer?]
- Actual: [What identity does the audience actually want permission for?]
- Gap: [Where is the mismatch?]

**Reframe:** [The identity trade that's actually being offered vs. what the product thinks it's offering]

**Ideas:**
- [Intervention strengthening the identity trade]
- [Intervention fixing a hero/mentor misalignment]
- [Intervention leveraging in-group social proof]

**Wild card:** [The identity dimension nobody is designing for — the one that makes this feel personal]
```

## What Good Output Looks Like

- The "I am" map is specific enough to feel like eavesdropping, not market research
- The gap between current and actual identity trade reveals a design opportunity, not just a branding tweak
- The backfire check names a specific anti-signal risk, not a vague "be careful"
- The wild card identifies an identity dimension the product hasn't considered — often the social or aspirational layer underneath the functional one
- Ideas change who the user *feels like*, not just what the product *does*
